The End of Chaucer's Tales Have an Unclear Resolution

The end of Chaucer’s tales do not have a clear resolution. This is because Chaucer left it up to the reader (in the tale) to have a “conversation” with what was written or said. Grudin states, “In all these closures, Chaucer moves consistently--and sometimes abruptly--from speaker to listener, performer to audience” (Grudin, 1162). This is meant to create communication and diversity within the endings developed by the reader’s individual interpretation of the reading. Grudin gives an example from Canterbury Tales, in the Pardoner’s Prologue. During this section of the book, the closure is simply achieved through the narrator’s comments.
