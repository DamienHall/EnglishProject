Lack of Closure Evokes Feelings of Dissatisfaction in Readers

Closure was deemed essential, in the late 1300’s, to end a piece of literature to provide the reader with a sense of satisfaction. Goeffery Chaucer, the author of Canterbury Tales, does not allow the reader to experience this. Instead, Chaucer purposefully invokes dissatisfaction and confusion in the reader. Michaela Grudin writes about this idea throughout her article Discourse and the Problem of Closure, which covers Chaucer’s interesting literary deviation. Many critics such as Elizabeth Salter and Piero Boitani find Chaucer’s “endings” unpleasurable. Grudin includes one of Boitani’s statements describing the lack of closure, “disturbing, unsatisfactory, ambiguous, problematic, incomplete conclusions” (Boitani, Piero).
